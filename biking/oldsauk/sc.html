I. Introduction/Statement of Interest

The Sierra Club is a national environmental organization. Its members are divided into chapters, which are subdivided again into Groups. The John Muir Chapter includes the Four Lakes Group, which represents some 2,700 members in Dane County and nearby communities.

The Sierra Club believes that reliance on the automobile as the primary means of transportation in this country and others has had profound negative impacts on the natural environment, as well as our social and cultural institutions. We advocate increased reliance on alternatives to the automobile as a means of transportation.

II. Sierra Club Involvement in the Current Controversy

Sierra Club representatives have spoken out in opposition to the proposal to expand Old Sauk Road into a four lane expressway. The Club remains opposed to the proposal. We believe that the City of Madison has been in no way responsive to our concerns and the proposal has not been changed in any way to mitigate its negative impacts. In fact, as we will describe later in this document, the City has made an apparently conscious effort to create impediments to public input on this project.

Sierra Club members live in and near the City of Madison and would be adversely affected, both directly and indirectly, by the completion of the project as proposed. Members and the organization as a whole claim standing for any litigation which may ensue from the outcome of this environmental review process.

III. The Environmental Assessment is Inadequate as a Matter of Law and Fact

The City's environmental asssessment (EA) fails to meet the minimum requirements of the Wisconsin Environmental Policy Act (WEPA) and the implementing regulations, ch. NR 150. The document fails to consider alternatives to the proposed action. Both alternative 1 and alternative 2 involve the construction of a 4-lane highway. They differ merely in the length of the proposed highway expansion.

The Club's objections to the project center on the project's expansion of highway capacity, in a manner certain to create environmental degradation, both direct and indirect. The failure to consider an alternative which involves reconstruction of the highway without creating additional lane capacity constitutes a failure of even the pretence of considering any genuine alternative to the proposed action.

IV. Failure to Provide Adequate Opportunity for Public Input.

Two hearings were held on the proposal, one in January 1999 and a second in May 1999. In each case the hearing was held near the site of the proposed highway expansion. Within a few days of the January hearing, a public meeting was held to discuss the City's proposal to expand Buckeye Road within the City's borders on the eastern portion of the City. We object to the fact that the City has an overall plan to expand nearly every road leaving the borders of the City into a four lane arterial. Notwithstanding that overall plan, the City has presented each of these proposals piecemeal, in a location inconvenient to all but immediate neighbors of the proposal. The Sierra Club is injured by this "shell game" approach to public input and involvement in the planning process. The Club provides helpful input and expertise on environmental and transportation policy. The City's efforts to hide each of its multiple highway expansion proposals under the guise of a local hearing for affected residents has tended to make it more difficult for us to participate in the public process affecting our City and region's transportation and land use future.

Moreover, statements were made by City staff at the January hearing that attempted to minimize the importance of public input on this project. Statements were made to the effect that "only" the bicycling community was opposed to the project. In addtion, statements were made to the effect that citizens were coming into the process "late", as the City's Engineering Department had plans for the construction of the highway for some years.

The environmental assessment itself was not made readily available to the public. The author of this document has only had a copy of the document for a few days, and has not had an adequate opportunity to review it. I was only able to obtain a copy through the help of an elected official.

V. The Proposed Highway Does Not Serve Legitimate Transportation Needs

The proposed highway goes from nowhere to nowhere. The highway goes a relatively short distance past the Beltline and then stops, resuming the 2 lane corridor which currently exists in the entire corridor. For this reason and for simple observation of the proposed right-of-way, the highway appears designed toserve specific localized developments west of the Beltline within areas currently within the City of Madison or designed to be annexed by the City for future development.

The area west of the Beltline near Old Sauk Road is one of the worst examples of sprawl within Madison City limits. South of Old Sauk Road is the strip mall which is anchored by the Target store. North of Old Sauk Road and immediately adjacent to the road is the Old Sauk Trails office park. Nearby is the John Q. Hammond Center - a convention center hotel complex within the City of Middleton which has attracted a fair amount of satellite development, all automobile-dependent and all pedestrian-unfriendly in its orientation. This area is an example of a failure of land use planning by the City of Madison. The current proposal is designed to run up a white flag and fuel additional sprawl development within City limits.

VI. The Environmental Assessment Fails to Review Cumulative Impacts on Madison's West Side

The Sierra Club is currently reviewing its options in its continuing opposition to plans by the Wisconsin Department of Transportation (WDOT) to construct a four lane expressway in the corridor of U.S. Highway 12 from Middleton to Sauk City. One of the potential obstacles to U.S. Highway 12 expansion is the fact that the highway will generate additional traffic and push the City into non-attainment status under the Federal Clean Air Act for certain pollutants. The west side of Madison is the "traffic shed" which receives a vast amount of traffic from currently sprawling suburbs north, west, and south of the City of Madison. With the construction of a four lane U.S. Highway 12, we believe that those suburbs will advance along a new front to the northwest which presently remains relatively rural.

The construction of a four lane Old Sauk Road would fuel this same sprawl. Old Sauk Road from the West Beltline to its western terminus at Timber Lane is becoming increasingly filled with massive single family homes - some refer to these as "execu-castles". These homes are generally built on single home, scatter-site lots, with a few small subdivisions mixed in. This is the proto-type of undesirable sprawl development within Dane County.

Parallel to Old Sauk Road there are several major roadways whose future is interdependent with that of Old Sauk. From north to south, these are: U.S. Highway 12, U.S. Highway 14, Old Sauk Road itself, Mineral Point Road, and Midtown Road. The cumulative effects of expanding each of these highways will create a vast amount of sprawl development. In turn, these developments and induced traffic - increased traffic which is generated as a direct result of the additional highway capacity - will have detrimental effects on the airshed of Madison's west side. We believe that any adequate review of the Old Sauk Road proposal would review the cumulative impacts of these various highway expansion proposals within the same traffic shed, and the impacts resulting on land use patterns in the surrounding areas, as well as air quality in the area.

The Midtown Road area is about to be impacted by the Hawk's Ridge subdivision. Although the City will annex this land, it is classic leapfrog sprawl, with its easternmost border being Highway M, east of which agricultural lands currently exist. The construction of this subdivision and golf course will reduce the likelihood of orderly development in the South High Point Road area, in which attempts were being made to design a neighborhood in the "new urbanism" style. Already City officials are backing away from promises to preserve greenspace, including the Ice Age Trail corridor, in this quadrant. The Old Sauk expansion will add to this westward pressure on Madison's expansion, drawing the line not at the Beltline but a mile or two west of the Beltline.

VII. The Environmental Assessment Fails to Adequately Address the City's Overall Program of Highway Expansion

The City of Madison has embarked upon a program to expand nearly every roadway heading out of town. These include, but are not limited to, the following: Old Sauk Road, Mineral Point Road, Midtown Road, S. High Point Road, Rimrock Road, Femrite Drive, Buckeye Road, Milwaukee St., Lein Rd, and Sprecher Road/Reiner Road.

This program of highway expansion speaks of a philosophy that the City of Madison is undergoing an economic expansion that will be unending, that the City should do everything in its power to fuel that expansion, and that the dominant means of transportation in the expansion areas should be the automobile.

We believe that this program has undesireable consequences, consequences which are certain to take place. First and foremost, the expansion will ensure the dominance of the automobile among our transportation mix. By ensuring that a four lane highway serves all real or imagined development, it becomes clear that the City's investment in transportation infrastructure will see to it that automobiles are served - often before the development the highways are designed to serve arive.

The existence of four lane highways is designed to generate additional traffic and additional development. Construction of highways in advance of development is a self-fulfilling prophecy - resulting in more traffic gridlock and more development.

In addition, four lane highways are intimidating and discourage all but the most stalwart bicyclists from leaving the City on two wheels. For example, the City of MacFarland is virtually inaccessible to Madison bicyclists because U.S. 51 where it leaves the City has so many lanes that bicyclists avoid this route for fear of their personal safety. Fish Hatchery Road, which has bicycle lanes for much of its distance, is avoided as well. The speed and dominance of automotive traffic keeps bicyclists away. Another example of a roadway with bicycle lanes which deters, rather than attracts bicyclists, is Mineral Point Road in the vicinity of West Towne Mall. Seminole Highway, by contrast, attracts bicyclists, because it is 2 automobile lanes in width, and thus is not as menacing as the larger roads.

As a matter of land use planning and transportation planning, the City should constrict arterials leaving City limits to two lanes. This way, development will be driven inward rather than outward and transportation solutions will need to be devised for development as it occurs, rather than highways propelling development further and further beyond existing City boundaries.

If the City believes that its plans to construct a four lane highway in the corridor of every cow path out of town, the City should provide the public with adequate public notice of its plans. The City should reveal its plans for each and every roadway our of town. The City should then conduct a comprehensive environmental review of these plans. Finally, the City should then conduct genuine public hearings, accessible and inviting to all citizens, including hearings held in the central part of the City of Madison, where so many citizens currently live.

VIII. The Old Sauk Road Proposal Would Have Detrimental Impacts on an Important Bicycle Transportation Corridor

The Old Sauk Road corridor is identified as an "urban escape route" by bicyclists statewide. The Bicycle Federation of Wisconsin has published detailed maps of the entire state. The map which depicts the Madison area shows Old Sauk Road as an escape route for bicyclists who wish to leave the City.

Expansion of the Old Sauk Road corridor to four lanes to serve outside-the-Beltline sprawl development would have a deleterious effect on this urban escape route. It would create the same impediment which currently exists at U.S. 51 and the South Beltline, making it nearly impossible to bicycle to McFarland from the City of Madison.

The City has contended that the creation of a four lane highway with bicycle lanes would actually increase safety for bicyclists. This has two fallacies of logic. One of these is that those attempting to cross the roadway would face greatly reduced safety. A recent article on the front page of the New York Times indicated that children today ride bicycles to a far lesser extent than children of previous generations. Their parents, and the children themselves, simply fear for their safety.

In addition, one of the advantages of openly soliciting public comment is that public officials may actually learn something from public comment. The massive, vociferous, and unanimous opposition to the Old Sauk Road expansion by members of the bicycling community may indicate an expertise on the part of the bicycling community from which City of Madison officials should willingly learn. The Sierra Club believes that this is the case.

Bicycling is not merely a recreational activity which is participated in by children and college students. In Madison, it has been a serious portion of the transportation mix for worktime commuting. The City should take this asset seriously and do everything in its power to make it easier for bicyclists to reach their worktime and other destinations by bicycle. Instead, the City appears to have rested on its laurels, disrupting bicycle routes in the central city and all but prohibiting bicycle riding on the outskirts of town. The notable exception is the new overpass near Verona Road, which, if adequately connected to bicycle arterials as planned, will make the southwestern portion of Madison south of the Beltline newly accessible to bicyclists. Successes like this should be expanded; four lane impediments at Beltline crossings will only serve to constrict bicycle passage to areas outside the central city.

IX. Impacts on Ice Age Reserve

The expansion of Old Sauk Road would have a negative impact on the Ice Age Reserve area between Madison and the Village of Cross Plains. This natural area is ecologically and geologically significant. It is the only remaining rural connection from central Madison to the driftless area. For many Madisonians, Cross Plains, Black Earth, and Spring Green become further away each year as traffic conditions worsen on Old Sauk Road and parallel routes.

By pushing the western boundary of Madison further west, attempts to expand the Ice Age Reserve will become more expensive. There are currently efforts to expand the Department of Natural Resources' land holdings in this area.

X. Secondary Land Use Impacts

As stated previously, the primary effect of the expansion of Old Sauk Road would be to generate sprawl development in the mold of the Target store development and other undesireable developments near the Old Sauk Road corridor. The meager gains which the "deal" relating to U.S. Highway 12 expansion won included one important principal - that WDOT must look at secondary land use impacts in considering the environmental impacts of its highway expansion projects. It is clear that a rigorous and impartial examination of the City of Madison's proposed expansion of Old Sauk Road would result in findings that construction of the road would have negative impacts in the form of secondary land use impacts. The Sierra Club believes that, to comply with its obligations under WEPA and ch. NR 150, the City of Madison is obligated to conduct a rigorous study of the secondary land use impacts of expansion of Old Sauk Road into a four lane arterial.

XI. Conclusions

The City of Madison's plans to expand Old Sauk Road are ill-advised and promise to have major negative impacts on our natural environment. The City has made a cursory attempt to appear to have addressed its obligations under WEPA through this environmental assessment. It has shown contempt for the concept of public participation and input. The City should ideally drop this damaging proposal and reassess its highway building program. In the alternative, it should embark upon a genuine environmental review of the Old Sauk Road proposal as well as its overall plan to create four lane highways everywhere a roadway leaves the City's boundaries.

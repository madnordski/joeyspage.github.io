Environment Decade Comment on Old Sauk Expansion

June 14, 1999
Mr. Rob Philips
City of Madison, Engineering Division
City County Building, Room 115
210 Martin Luther King Jr. Blvd.
Madison, WI 53710




Mr. Philips:

We are writing in regards to the Environmental Assessment for the Proposed Improvement of Old Sauk Road Between Prairie Smoke Road and the West Beltline. Please accept these comments as part of the official record.

We object to the four lane divided roadways proposed in Alternative I and Alternative II, for Old Sauk Road and Pleasant View Road to four lane divided roadways for the following reasons:

  1. The new road capacity is not justified by current or projected traffic volume in this corridor.

  2. New and expanded road miles are enablers of sprawl, or low density, auto-dependent residential and commercial development.

  3. The plan does not adequately consider the direct and secondary impacts that the increased road capacity will have on land use. Increased access and traffic volume have historically had drastic effects on neighborhoods and communities where similar expansions have occurred.

  4. Expanded roadways are known to generate induced traffic, or new traffic which is diverted from other routes, and from new trips related to new development accessed by the expanded roadway.

  5. The environmental impacts of the expansion include increased air and water pollution, loss of farmland, development of ridge tops, and fragmentation and loss of habitat.

  6. The safety of bicyclists and pedestrians will be jeopardized by the higher traffic volumes and increased vehicle speeds, and by the vastly increased width of the road. The sidewalks in the plan are not designed with the walker's safety in mind, i.e. to minimize the number of times a walker must cross the roadway, and to have sidewalks along all sections of the road.

Furthermore, the proposed expansion is in direct contrast to Dane County land use plans, particularly Design Dane!, which emphasize protecting rural farmland, and protecting the integrity of rural communities. Dane County Executive Kathleen Falk has strongly urged the City to find an alternative to the four-lane proposal.

Madison typically seeks to insulate neighborhoods from the higher traffic volume and speeds, noise pollution, and increased truck traffic associated with business parks and other commercial activities. Since the Citys development plan calls for primarily residential development in the area, this four-lane expansion is inconsistent and inappropriate.

For Madison community residents, Old Sauk Road represents the only two-lane access to rural areas south of the Beltline. This is especially important to bicyclists, who rely on the route as the safest and quickest route out of the city. It is designated by WisDOT on the Wisconsin State Bicycle Map as an Urban Escape Route. Indeed, it is heavily used by bicyclists, and functions as an important link in the usable network of roadways and bikeways that provide intercity linkages for bicycles.

The two-lane route is an important gateway for tourists and recreational visitors who are most interested in our natural beauty and local sporting opportunities. It is possible that the road become a Rural Scenic Byway in the future, an alternative that local residents and the greater Madison community have expressed interest in.

For business interests, the ability to attract high-demand employees depends in large part on the livability of the area. A significant and growing number of commuters choose to bicycle to work. Preserving the rural character of the area directly and indirectly accessible from Old Sauk Rd. is critical to maintaining our quality of life, and the quality work environment that many businesses seek in a location.

We recommend as an alternative that Old Sauk Road remain a two-lane configuration with turning lanes. The section of Old Sauk Road from heartland Trail to Prairie Smoke Road should be an improved two-lane roadway with shoulders and bike lanes, and with sidewalks on both sides. Traffic calming measures such as medians, traffic circles, sidewalk bulb-outs, and landscaping, should be used to reduce vehicle speeds and discourage diverted traffic.

We appreciate your thorough consideration of these comments.

Sincerely,

Keith Reopelle
Program Director

Subject: S.O.S. -- Save Old Sauk
Date: Mon, 09 Nov 1998 00:30:47 -0600
From: Joseph King
To: bikies@cycling.org

Thanks to all who called their alderperson regarding the expansion of Old Sauk Road to four lanes. The net result at the Pedestrian/Bicycle/Motor Vehicle Commission (PBMVC) was to delay the decision. It was suggested to me recently that this will be put before the PBMVC for a decision at their November meeting instead. I'm concerned that despite registering interest, we have not been notified of any planned public meetings on the issue before the PBMVC makes a decision.

Unfortunately, I was not able to attend the discussion that did occur over Old Sauk at the last PBMVC meeting because I had to leave to attend another meeting. I have some idea of what the issues are so I will attempt to list them here. Please feel free to add to the discussion points below.

I would suggest calling your alderperson *every* Monday to ask about Old Sauk road and urge them to push for 2-lanes with room for bikes instead of 4-lanes for trucks and Beltline traffic.

Reasons for turning Old Sauk Road into yet another four laner:

  1. Future residential plans and the business park are creating traffic demands on Old Sauk so four lanes are needed.
    ANS: Business Park traffic should be directed to the Beltline. We shouldn't be funneling truck and through traffic toward existing and planned neighborhoods. When construction early this year disconnected Old Sauk from the Beltline, business park users and local residents could take a short detour to Old Sauk. This reduced traffic dramatically. Clearly the business park doesn't need Old Sauk heading west or generate as much traffic as we are lead to believe. This suggests that much of the traffic is driving through the area to Mineral Point or U.S. 14.

  2. Why should we keep traffic capacity down on Old Sauk for a few recreational cyclists?
    ANS: Cyclists are not the issue. The benefits of maintaining the rural character of at least one route to the driftless area accrue to the entire community. In the past year I've interviewed a number of job candidates and in most instances the cycling in and around Madison has turned out to be important to them. Cycling is an indicator of a safe and healthy environment. This has helped us attract some of the best people in the field. The fact that you can live in the city and still be connected to the rural surroundings is as important as the farmer's market to many Madison residents.

  3. With four lanes there'll be bike lanes.
    ANS: You can have bike lanes with two lanes. In fact, it was stated that there would only be one sidewalk because of the limited width. With two lanes there would be room for bike lanes and two sidewalks. Old Sauk should look more like Seminole Highway not Fish Hatchery Road!

  4. Traffic on Old Sauk is already higher than Seminole Highway so two lane improvements won't work.
    ANS: Traffic is high because improvements are needed at the Mineral Point interchange. We should discourage cut-through traffic not encourage more with four lanes of pavement stretching up the hill toward the horizon. If this volume requires 4-lanes and volumes are increasing on Seminole Highway then we'll have to build that road out to four lanes some time soon. Will it ever stop?

  5. There's nothing special about Old Sauk, this is just the normal development pattern.
    ANS: Old Sauk road is the only remaining rural connection from central Madison to the driftless area. For cyclists, Cross Plains, Black Earth, and Spring Green become further away each year conditions worsen on Old Sauk Road. For everyone else, Madison inches closer to the ubiquitous urban sprawl of Chicagoland. Cyclists are, if you will, just the indicator species.

  6. Having bike lanes with four lanes is better than no improvement at all.
    ANS: This is a threat not a choice. Conditions on Old Sauk demand improvement because space is limited and visibility is variable. A slightly wider two lane road is all that is needed.

  7. Four Lanes with bike lanes will make things better than they are now.
    ANS: By encouraging traffic to leave the Beltline at Old Sauk instead of Mineral Pt. or US14 some of the traffic will take Old Sauk all of the way to Timber Lane. As unpleasant as four lanes of fast traffic can be, increasing the traffic on the entire length of Old Sauk will destroy the rural character of the entire road.

  8. Cyclists can cut through the business park and take Blackhawk Rd.
    ANS: But this road doesn't go through -- it just requires the cyclist to rejoin a now busier Old Sauk Road down the road a bit.

  9. Improving Old Sauk to four lanes is needed because of existing traffic.
    ANS:It follows then that four lanes are way overdue on Mineral Point, US14, and US12 since all of these roads see a lot more traffic than Old Sauk. Airport Road is already planned for four lanes. Is our plan to distribute through traffic out onto our rural roads?

  10. Old Sauk isn't needed for cycling.
    ANS: The State of Wisconsin has designated Old Sauk Road as bicycle escape route. A two lane improved Old Sauk Road will, once again, be the most direct and pleasant route to the driftess area of Wisconsin. For Sunday drives with the family or a cycle trip to Riley, Cross Plains, or Black Earth -- we must reclaim Old Sauk Road.

  11. Four lanes of traffic won't bother cyclists at all.
    ANS:Compare the number of cyclists on Fish Hatchery Road (4-lanes with bike lanes) to Seminole Highway and you tell me if 4-lanes or 2-lanes are better for cyclists. Better yet, get on a bike and find out why.

  12. We should fill in the gaps of rural area around the city before expanding outward.
    ANS:The County Executive has proposed that we keep some rural roads as rural as possible. These fingers of the country reaching into the city will make the city a better place to live. Old Sauk is such a rural route.

  13. Old Sauk is a lost cause as a route for cyclists.
    ANS: It is only neglected not lost. With two-lane improvements and improvements near the Mineral Point/Beltline interchange, traffic volumes on Old Sauk can be reduced. A recent closure of a part of Old Sauk Road tells us that the vast majority of the traffic on Old Sauk is not local.

  14. If you oppose four lanes for Old Sauk, you oppose growth.
    ANS: Much to the contrary. If Madison hopes to promote high-tech business, maintaining a quality living environment in Madison is vital. High-tech businesses like the Genetics Computer Group compete for employees with firms on the east and west coasts. Madison is attractive because of it's reputation as a quality city and the fame of the local cycling scene.

    S.O.S.

Road-widening plan doesn't please cyclists

by Matt Mullins, Wisconsin State Journal
January 20, 1999

Advocates and residents
worried plans for Old Sauk
would make it too busy and
hazardous. Traffic officials
say there's too much traffic
already.

A draft off the street drew the wind and cold into a West Side realty office last week, when about 50 residents and bike advocates offered a chilly reception to city plans for the expansion of Old Sauk Road.

"I'm not sure we changed a lot of minds tonight," admitted City Engineer Larry Nelson after the Jan. 13 meeting. Nelson and the city Department of Transportation used maps, photos and their rationale in an effort to describe the widening of Old Sauk Road from two to four lanes between Excelsior Drive and Prairie Smoke Road just west of the Beltline. Car lanes 11 feet wide would be flanked by 4-foot bicycle lanes under the proposal.

Residents lamented the likelihood of a fast, loud thoroughfare, and bike advocates protested that the development of one of the last safe bikeways out of the city to the surrounding countryside would artificially inflate vehicle use. City officials insisted there's already too much traffic for the road.

The project won't break ground until at least next year. According to city projections, roughly 15,000 vehicles came off the Beltline onto to Old Sauk Road a day last year. (State DOT guidelines suggest a four-lane, divided road for traffic of about 17,000 vehicles a day.) By 2020, the city expects daily traffic on Old Sauk to jump to 36,000 to 42,000. Demand warrants immediate expansion, Nelson said.

"We have a two-lane (road) now, and we have a lot of demand (for expansion) from the businesses and people who work within the area."

Residents, though, wondered if a four lane highway, even with its planned bike lanes, would preclude the use of bike routes by children, or the ease of crossing for residents slowed by age or illness. Others noted that the character of the neighborhood would change, discouraging single-family housing; they pointed out that pedestrian and family-friendly neighborhoods, such as along parts of Regent Street, are quickly vanishing in Madison.

"We are unique, " said resident Julie Kreunen. "I just wonder if there are ways of developing this without (expanding to a four-lane)."

Neighbor Ray Tomlinson echoed this concern. "It's serving the interests of development, and not necessarily the interests of the citizen," he said.

Bike advocates compared the change to other roads in the city, such as Mineral Point Road and Fish Hatchery roads, both of which have diamond lanes for use by buses, bikes and right-turning vehicles. But few cyclists are bold enough to use them.

One advocate, Joe King, said city estimates for Old Sauk's projected usage may be naively low. He pointed out that Old Sauk rises to the crest of a hill off the Beltline. "When you look to the west, you'll see the straight line to the horizon, " he said. The will be too inviting for rush-hour drivers, he claimed.

Advocates pointed to Seminole Highway as a road with similar traffic needs that avoided expansion and remained neighborhood and biker-friendly. Nelson countered that the city of Fitchburg had to adjust the road. "Fitchburg expanded the area south of PD into a four-lane road," he said.

Jeanne Hoffman, executive director of the Bicycle Federation of Wisconsin, asked if federal funding of 80 percent of the expansion was too much for the city Department of Transportation to resist.

Tomlinson concurred. "I would suggest that the enticement of the federal money for this is an overdose," he said.

After the meeting, Nelson disputed the linkage of road expansion with future development. "We are not in advance of development," he insisted. He also disputed the proposed expansion's impact on bikers. "A lot of people use Old Sauk Road for recreation purposes. They're not on the road during the (week)days."

Nelson doesn't sound inclined to budge on plans, but DOT officials insisted nothing is finalized. City engineers will review plans following a meeting at 7 p.m. Jan. 25 at M&I Bank, 7801 Mineral Point Road, for the Walnut Grove neighborhood association. (A meeting for the Blackhawk neighborhood was held Tuesday night.) Following the meeting, KL Engineering, a consulting firm retained by the city, will submit an environmental impact study to state and federal authorities in March. A 30-day public comment period will begin, and a final proposal will emerge.

KL's project manager Todd Hertz seemed perplexed at the bicyclists' protest. He noted that Old Sauk, with its two lanes and narrow shoulders, hardly suits safe bicycling currently. "There's nowhere to ride. I feel we're improving it for them," he said.

Tomilnson seemed unconvinced. "They can make this highway safe and serviceable without doing all of this."

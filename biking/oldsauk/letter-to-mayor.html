S.O.S. - Save Old Sauk
Mayor Sue Bauman
Mayor's Office
Room 403 City-County Building

Dear Mayor Bauman,

We realize that there is more to being mayor than listening to residents complain about traffic. We also realize that the fate of one road can seem insignificant in light of the many issues you face. It seems to us, however, that as Mayor of Madison you are ultimately responsible for how Madison represents itself and how it conducts business with the public.

It is common for cities of the size of Madison to sprawl and suburbanize. Many do so at the request of developers. Cities like Boulder, Colorado that develop clear and enforceable plans for growth are rare. And even when neighborhood plans call for traffic calming and a curb on sprawl, these plans can be undermined by business-as-usual approaches. When this occurs, it takes strong leadership to bring things back under control.

Such leadership is needed badly in the case of Old Sauk Road. We say this based on the somewhat biased and misleading presentations of the Old Sauk Expansion Plan witnessed at two public meetings. The aspects of these presentations that are undeniably misleading are well documented in a Wisconsin State Journal article which is enclosed.

We call these aspects of the presentation to your attention:

  1. City staff claims that the traffic levels on Old Sauk Road are already high enough to justify four lane expansion. They cite a traffic count of 15,000 west of the beltline as justification for expansion.

    What they don't say is that the traffic count measurement they are referring to was taken on a portion of Old Sauk Road that has already been widened to 4 lanes. City records show that on the section of Old Sauk Road slated for expansion, the traffic counts recorded were only 5,233 at the time the 15,000 measurement was made. They also fail to mention that on Seminole Highway near the Beltine they recorded counts in excess of 10,000. This two-lane road serves both motorists and cyclists well.

  2. The city plans are labeled as "Alternative 1" and "Alternative 2," yet city staff speaks of these as "phase 1" and "phase 2" of "the project."

    Larry Nelson did at least say to the meeting participants that the city expects to build the project in these two phases because of delays caused by historic property regulations. You should direct staff to re-label these drawings to reflect this apparent intent. It is misleading to call either an alternative when it is clear they are simply two phases of the same plan.

  3. Presentations of the plan highlight the bike lane as improving conditions for cyclists.

    Staff and Alderperson Hamblin insist that providing bike lanes on a four-lane Old Sauk Road will improve conditions for bicyclists, despite the fact that large numbers of cyclists have expressed their displeasure with this plan. They seem unable to recognize that the least used bicycle facilities in Madison are bike lanes on four lane roads. Mineral Point Road, and Fish Hatchery Road are two outstanding examples of bike lanes that are useful for access to the businesses along those roads but avoided otherwise.

  4. Presentations attempt to further justify the road expansion using the business park and the neighborhood plan.

    This is, in our view, both misleading and inconsistent with past practices. Since the neighborhood plan calls for this area to be mostly residential, shouldn't the approach be more like that taken with the UW Research Park which is located in a residential area? In that case, every effort was made to isolate the neighborhood from the daily traffic of the park. In addition, it ignores that fact that the park as several major and minor entrances with 10 lanes leading to and from the Beltline. If the business park's need was so great, how is it that the traffic levels at Old Sauk Road and Pleasant View are as low as 6,000? Widening this road to four lanes assures there will be more traffic on Old Sauk Road all the way to Timber Lane. It will also increase pressure on sections of Old Sauk east of Gammon Road that have enjoyed steady traffic levels for six years or more. Finally it will further increase pressure to suburbanize near-by rural areas.

Like Seminole Highway, Old Sauk road serves residents of Madison by providing a leisurely alternative to Mineral Point and US14 for drives in the country or rides on a bike. Like Seminole Highway, Old Sauk Road is slated to serve a mostly residential area. Old Sauk Road should merely compliment the major roads to the north and south.

Old Sauk Road is in need of improvement as it is unusually narrow, has no shoulders, and in need of repair. With traffic levels of about 6,000 trips per day on the section of Old Sauk Road proposed for expansion, a two lane improvement is far more appropriate. Even the State of Wisconsin Department of Transportation guidelines do not call for four-lane divided roads until traffic reaches 17,000 trips or greater.

It is important to bear in mind that this past fall's elections featured numerous referenda on sprawl and highway expansion. In almost all cases, the controled-growth referenda won and controled-growth candidates beat incumbents. The traffic projections Traffic Engineering makes for Old Sauk Road are based on the development strategies that have resulted in accelerated sprawl which has plagued our nation since World War II. Should a neighborhood focused plan finally prevail for Old Sauk, the projected traffic on that road will never come to pass. But widening to four lanes now in order to prepare for a yet more sprawl will assure that Old Sauk generates much more bypass traffic than it would if it remained two lanes. This will close off one more "escape route" into the rural areas of the county. And we remind you that two unnecessary four-lane expansions (on Buckeye and Femrite) are threatening escape routes on the east side of town as well.

As Mayor we kindly ask that you direct staff to develop "real" alternatives that are more consistent with traffic levels and the neighborhood plan.

Sincerely,

Mike Barrett, Darin Burleigh, Jeanne Hoffman, Joseph King, Scott Rose, Mark Shahan, Robbie Webber, Tim Wong

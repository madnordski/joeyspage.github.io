Date: October 26, 1998
To: Pedestrian, Bicycle, Motor Vehicle Commission
Re: Plans For Old Sauk Road Widening
From: Commission Member, Mark Shahan

I and other bicyclists have serious concerns about expanding Old Sauk Road to four lanes west of the Beltline. It isn't that we don't think improvements should be made. On the contrary, we think improvements such as bike lanes, a wide curb lane, or paved shoulders, and sidewalks are long overdue. What we object to is the creation of a four lane divided highway on such an important bike route. Instead of a 4 lane divided highway, we propose a two lane divided road akin to Tokay Boulevard. Our concerns with creating yet another four lane highway out of Madison are listed below.

The Bicyclist's View

A bicyclist does not perceive a route to be bicycle friendly just because it has bike lanes. Conversely, roads without bike lanes are not necessarily inhospitable to bicyclists. Other factors such as the amount of motor vehicle traffic, the speed of the motor vehicles, the space with which motor vehicles have to pass a cyclist, and the number of lanes of traffic all are important factors in the bike friendliness of a road.

A four lane road flunks three of four of these criteria even with bike lanes. Posted speeds, let alone actual speeds, are higher on four lane roads making it difficult for cyclist to merge with traffic to perform normal traffic maneuvers such as left turns. Motor vehicles simply approach from behind too quickly for most cyclists to feel safe when they need to move across or into a lane(s) of traffic. Because of multiple lanes, cyclists have to cross two or more high speed traffic lanes instead of one when turning left. Four lane roads have more traffic making it difficult to find gaps in traffic to perform these maneuvers. Moreover, the biking experience can, at best, be described as unpleasant along a noisy, congested, multiple lane highway even if you never leave the bike lane. The result is that the average person avoids these roads like the plague even if they are inclined to bike and even when there are bike lanes. The Mineral Point Road bike lanes are an excellent example of this phenomenon. They are not used as much as they should be because most people simply are not comfortable riding on a road that busy.

Old Sauk is the ONLY Bike Route in this area

Old Sauk Road is listed as an official bike route east of the Beltline by the City of Madison, and as an escape route out of the City west of the Beltline in the State of Wisconsin Bike Plan and on the State Bike Map. It is in fact the ONLY bike route that crosses the Beltline in this part of the City. Hence the need to keep Old Sauk Bike friendly.

Mineral Point Road on the east side of the Beltline has bike lanes but they do extend to the west side of the Beltline. Without bike lanes or paved shoulders, Mineral Point Road is a death trap for bicyclists. The other two crossings of the Beltline in this area are Greenway Boulevard which requires a one mile detour one way, and US Highway 14 which is even a further detour and is another four lane divided highway. There has also been talk of constructing a pedestrian/bicycle overpass of the Beltline south of Old Sauk. However, this project is low on the funding list and probably won't be completed for many years.

Connections, Connections

Because Old Sauk Road is the only bike route that crosses the Beltline, it is the only route that can provide bicycle access to the rest of the City for the Junction Ridge and Blackhawk subdivisions. If the City is serious about providing multi-modal transportation for the whole City, with providing real transportation options for people that people will use, Old Sauk must be as bike friendly as possible and this means it cannot be four lanes. A four lane Old Sauk will act as a barrier to most people who wish to bike and will effectively deny them a bicycle connection to the rest of the city.

Old Sauk is also a gateway to what Bicycling magazine called some of the best recreational biking in the country, recreational biking that helped rank Madison the 4th most bike friendly city in North America. With the increase in traffic that has already occurred on Old Sauk, recreational cyclists such as Bombay Bicycle Club have started to avoid Old Sauk.

Old Sauk will also be important for providing bike and pedestrian connections between far west side neighborhoods. Junction Ridge is a natural destination for people living in the Blackhawk subdivision and they ought to be able to easily bike and walk there.

Diversion of Traffic from Mineral Point and Highway 14

As Mineral Point becomes more congested, particularly around the entrances to Menards and Target, people will "discover" Old Sauk if it is expanded to four lanes. People will detour down Pleasant View and Timber Lane to use the nice new four lane Pleasant View and Old Sauk intersection rather than the mess at Junction Road/Mineral Point. The same holds true for US Highway 14. In this way, Old Sauk will induce demand that will quickly fill it to capacity. Cars have another way to get to the far west side, bikes and pedestrians don't.

Speeding

The PBMVC has spent a lot of time dealing with issues of speeding and what to due about it. We recently unanimously approved an ordinance allowing reduced street widths because narrower streets have clearly been shown to reduce speeds. We have also approved neighborhood traffic management projects such as traffic circles in an attempt to reduce speeding. A four lane road is counterproductive to these efforts. A four lane road looks like an interstate and people drive on them like they are interstates. Speeds on most four lane roads are simply not compatible with bicycling. A heavy police presence is then necessary to try to reduce speeding and this incurs a heavy financial cost to be effective. We should take a hint from the Street width ordinance and leave Old Sauk a two lane road.

Impact on the city core

Tonight we are also wrestling with approval of Lot 17 and earlier this year we fretted about making North Park Street more pedestrian friendly. These issues are related to widening peripheral roads to multi-lane highways. The easier we make it to drive into Madison, the more we will be faced in the central city with unpleasant decisions such as building more parking lots, congested streets, and streets not built on a human scale. We cannot build our way out of our traffic woes. People will never chose a mode other than the car if we always make make it easier to drive and don't provide real alternatives that are safe, convenient, and enjoyable.

Cost

These widenings also carry a big price tag and not just for the construction. People often say, "This is funded 80% by federal funds. Why not take advantage of federal money." The problem is the Feds don't pay for operational costs, property tax payers do. I would like to remind commissioners that in a 1994 study, the City estimated that 37% of property tax revenue for the City was spent dealing with motor vehicles. That is one eighth of a Madisonians total property tax bill. This cost not only includes increased construction and maintenance but increased police, emergency response, street cleaning, and lighting costs as well. What is especially upsetting to me is that peripheral road expansions primarily benefit people outside Madison but Madison tax payers pick up the tab for destroying their own quality of life. Old Sauk Road is particularly irksome in this regard because of the poor land use practices in the Town of Middleton that are "necessitating" this expansion.

Public Hearings

Bicyclists were also promised public hearings on this project in an e-mail message by Larry Nelson (see attached enclosure). Bicyclists were never informed of any hearings if they took place. The least that should be done is to have these hearings so bicyclist input can be heard before the designs are approved. Otherwise, the input is meaningless. I contrast this to the nine months of work and hearings for the Southwest Commuter Bike and Pedestrian Path by a citizens committee on which I have served. It seems that bike projects require such hearings but major road construction does not. I think we have things backwards. Maybe if we had citizens committees for major road expansions, we would be much slower to develop multi-lane highways, have a more balanced transportation system, save a lot of money.

Based on the above concerns, bicyclists propose that we build a two lane divided road with bike lanes, sidewalks, and turn lanes. We believe this will visually narrow the appearance of the road decreasing speeds and discouraging this route from being used as a detour for Mineral Point Road and US Highway 14. This is not such a strange idea. It is exactly what many on this Commission have proposed and voted for on US Highway 12. Old Sauk Road is to the west side what Seminole Highway is to the south side. Seminole Highway also serves heavy commuter traffic and crosses the Beltline. Yet it still functions quite well as a two lane road with bike lanes. This is what bicyclists want for Old Sauk Road.

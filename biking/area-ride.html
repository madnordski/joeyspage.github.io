Road Closed: It's a Good Sign

In the spring of '98 this sign helped to improve
the cycling conditions around Madison.

It was thirty minutes before my morning meeting at work and I was about twenty minutes away on Fitchrona Road when I saw the sign, "Road Closed." If I had to backtrack now I'd be late for sure. Soon after passing the barricades the pavement gave way to gravel and then packed dirt. It turned out to be quite bikeable and I soon found myself turning onto Nesbit Road. As if to reward my venture, the road closure limited traffic on the normally busy Nesbit Road to just a trickle. What I just described occurred during the spring of 1998.

Among the numerous road closures around Madison that spring there were a couple that opened up new routes for cyclists and one that returned an old favorite. Although road closures have forced me to backtrack on more than one occasion, these closures were pretty good for cycling at least temporarily.

Old Sauk Road was closed just west of the Beltline Highway since early that spring. On one nice day in February, before the closure, this route seemed lost to cyclists. Cars passing too quickly and too frequently made it difficult to enjoy this first taste of spring and the rural character of the road. A month later the same stretch of road was pleasant and the motorists were overwhelmingly polite. It is amazing how such a large effect could be obtained by simply disconnecting this road from the beltline. This seems to indicate that people have been using Old Sauk as an alternative to US 14 and Mineral Point Road. It also demonstrates that there is at least one way to keep Old Sauk Road a rural escape route from Madison.

Joe Frost from Rockford, Illinois makes his way through the
barricades at State Highway 92 and Spring Rose Road.

Madison cyclists enjoyed other road closures along popular cycling routes that spring and summer. County M just east of Verona was closed most of the spring. This makes a popular connection between Paoli and Madison even nicer. Since the closure was announced to motorists back where M intersects County PB, traffic from the Range Trail intersection to the Whalen Road intersection was unusually light. The closure was east of the Whalen Road intersection so the cyclist had this section of County M all to herself.

Another road cyclists had all to themselves was State Highway 69. There was a bridge out between Schaum and Locust. If you were heading west from Paoli toward Fritz Rd., you'd normally take Speedway to Schaum Road. But that summer you could enjoy taking Highway 69 to Schaum. This makes for less climbing and it was a very nice ride because, except for the odd tractor, there was absolutely no traffic on this section of the state highway. On the other side of the impassible bridge, highway 69 made for a great connection between Riverside and Locust.

It is kind of fun to bike on state highways like 69 that are narrow, don't have paved shoulders, and normally see plenty of traffic. In almost identical fashion state highway 92, which was closed just east of Mount Vernon, made for a fun connector to Spring Rose Road from the west.

It's too late now to get out and enjoy all these closed roads because most of the road work has been completed. Instead, now would be a good time to write your representatives suggesting that it would be good for all of Dane County to keep the rural character of at least a few roads around Madison. You might ask the Madison Mayor and the County Executive if using Old Sauk Road to reduce congestion on the Beltline, Mineral Point Road, and U.S. Highway 14 really is the best use of this rural road. The City of Madison apparently thinks it is because they are working on plans to expand Old Sauk to four lanes.

by Joseph King

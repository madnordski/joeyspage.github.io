Pie Ride from Cross Plains to Plain

by Joseph King

It was a difficult choice to make. I had to decide whether to bike out to Blue Mound State Park and help out the Bicycle Federation of Wisconsin or get in a more challenging training ride for this year's Ride the Rockies. In past years the ride to Plain for pie has marked the beginning of our more demanding training rides. This year the El Niño weather pattern has given us an unusually mild spring and I've logged more training miles than ever. One of my training buddies, however, felt he was due for a long ride so I sat down and mapped out a route to Plain.

The route would take us through four counties. We would take in views of bluffs, wild flowers and turkey while keeping an eye out for albino deer. The cue sheet below starts and ends in Cross Plains, however, we would each start from our homes on this pleasant Saturday morning. Due to thunder storms late in the day I hitched a ride back home from Black Earth making it a 97 mile trip for me.

Our Saturday training group varies from a few riders to a half dozen. Because of my indecision, the email notice went out to the group late on Friday so I wasn't surprised that it would be only Jack and I today. The air was clear and cool as we started out from Cross Plains on County Road KP.

The ride north to Roxbury was extraordinarily nice on this quite county road. For the most part, the hay along this route had been cut and rolled up but in one spot near the road it was being cut. The smell of fresh hay was so penetrating that I could taste it. Strangely enough at that moment I felt as if I could eat hay but I'm sure my single stomach would object so I pedal on.

Leaving the county road for Old Settlers and Dunlap Hollow results in our first significant climb of the day. Near the bottom of this wonderful hill is a family of cyclists. Mom's bike is outfitted with a pedal bike attachment for a seven year old cyclist and dad is pulling a trailer with toddler in tow. Jack asks if they are going to continue up this hill and we are unable to suppress our chuckles of admiration. Dad calls out in response, "I don't like the sound of that!" Indeed, it is a long and beautiful climb up Dunlop Hollow toward it's crossing with Hwy 12. The rocks and wildflowers cling to the roads edge and the vistas reveal mostly wooded hill tops with the eventual view of the Wisconsin River cradling the town of Sauk City below.

The downside of Dunlop Hollow is fast and straight. At the bottom we manage to cross Highway 12 and make our way into the sleepy village of Roxybury and then north to Highway 60 where we cross the river and head west pushed by a light but steady tail wind. Our route west is along a once popular bike trail that is part of a route that connects La Crosse to Chicago. Perhaps I should use the past tense here because this route is not even indicated on the new State Bike Maps now available from the Bicycle Federation of Wisconsin. On my 1986 map it indicates that this trail forms a loop on Cty PF from Sauk City north to Baraboo, Devils Lake State Park and south again through Merriac returning to Sauk City. Jack recalls that this part of the route was once promoted as a bicycle route that Chicagoans could use by taking the train to La Crosse and biking back home. As we're biking along we notice a set of tall shade trees with a sign underneath that reads, "Bikers Rest."

Continuing west we pass a nature preserve and consider for a moment heading north toward Natural Bridge State Park as we have in the past. This adds another long and beautiful climb culminating with views of the Baraboo Hills region but I nix the idea thinking that we'll each bike about 100 miles if we just stick to the planned route. Our planned route keeps us heading west on town roads after we leave cty PF. It is along one of these roads that we scared up a cock turkey sitting near the roads' edge. His large wings seem barely large enough to get his massive body off the ground.

I'm ready to eat, as usual, by the time reach Plain. With a meager breakfast and over fifty miles showing on my odometer, this is no surprise. The fare at Plain Jane's Cafe in Plain is typical of small town diners in Wisconsin. Except for the occasional melt, you won't find fancy California sandwiches on this menu. In fact, the lunch menu is almost exclusively sandwiches and the prices are rock bottom. Making this place an excellent value for your basic sandwich, slaw and fries. The coleslaw today is make from chopped not shredded cabbage in a very light dressing. It is mostly the crisp inner white leaves of cabbage with just a few shreds of carrot. I had the tuna salad and Jack the BLT which we inhaled with fries.

Timing is everything when it comes to pie. Small diners like Jane's, in order to have fresh pie, don't make a lot of pie so what you get depends on what is available. Some places in the interest of selection, make much more pie than they sell. Consequently, you'll all to often get pie that tastes more like the refrigerator than like fruit. Today Jane's serves us a delicious apple pie. The fruit is solid and the full crust is flaky. There is plenty of cinnamon and not too much syrup surrounding the apples.

After the pie Jane tells us about an albino deer wandering around the area. She suggests a route east of here where we might come upon it. The route she indicated includes a steep climb up a ridge and is well off our planned route so we forego it. Maybe next time.

Our return trip across the Wisconsin River valley is more beautiful than expected. With long views of bluffs standing in a row overlooking an expanse of farm fields and the river beyond. Along this route we're treated to the lovely blue flowers of the Cow Vetch.

Crossing the river we enter Iowa county which seems to guarded by tall standing sentries of Cow Parsnip each wearing a white beret. We avoid one of the longest and steepest climbs in Wisconsin and head south on the bike path to County T. It gradually climbs past lovely stands of Columbine interlaced with the flowers of the Wild Rose.

The climbs, views, and descents in Iowa County are spectacular but the sky is becoming threatening. Thunder starts to boom so we waste no time on the ridge tops and we soon find ourselves back in Dane County. By the time we get to Black Earth rain is falling and the lightning seems to be closing in. We find refuge in Black Earth and I find a ride home.

With only two weekends of training before we leave for Colorado, it will be hard to top this ride. We took in the varied terrain of Columbia, Sauk, Iowa, and Dane counties and enjoyed our visit to Plain Jane's Cafe. I feel like I'm ready now. After the ride I was not longing for a nap. And today, the day after my longest ride of the season, I feel like I could go out again.

Report of the Committee for the Study of the Southwest Commuter Bicycle and Pedestrian Path

 

Charge of the Committee

A citizens committee was appointed by the Mayor to collect information about the proposed Southwest Rail Line Bicycle and Pedestrian Path from adjacent homeowners and potential users. The four-mile path would be on the abandoned Wisconsin & Calumet rail corridor. It would start at Randall Road (near Camp Randall) and head southwest to Lovell Lane (approximately a mile south of the Beltline). The committee was chosen to represent the interests of adjacent homeowners, bikers, walkers, dog walkers, cross-country skiers, and other interested parties. It has been holding public meetings and collecting information since February. Based on this information, the committee developed the following set of recommendations and goals for the path.

 

Introduction

The quality of life in Madison has repeatedly been rated as among the best of any city in the country. One of the reasons often cited for this quality of life is the 25 miles of bike and pedestrian paths in Madison. Thus, ease of walking and biking are seen as indicators of the quality of life in a city. Being able to walk and bike in and between neighborhoods makes the city more livable.

In Madison, the bike and pedestrian paths provide many benefits to the community. These paths are used by bicyclists, pedestrians, runner/joggers, disabled users, in-line skaters, and other users. They provide inexpensive, clean recreation as well as important commuter routes. They provide safe routes for children going to school, play, and neighbors, obviating the need for parents to drive drive them. They provide continuity of the pedestrian and bicycle network by connecting parts of the city otherwise cut off by major roads that act as barriers to bicyclists and pedestrians. They encourage people to bike and walk who are otherwise intimidated by traffic and, thus, help slow the growth in congestion and pollution. They reclaim public spaces for public use, and in the process beautify the space with landscaping and by removing trash and unsightly vegetation. They allow ever yone including the elderly and disabled to enjoy the natural surroundings of the path. And they tend to increase the value of homes near the paths.

Many of these benefits will accrue to the proposed Southwest Commuter Bicycle and Pedestrian Path if and when it is constructed. This path will connect the southwestern part of the city, which is cut off by Verona Road and the Beltline, to the rest of Madison's pedestrian and bicycle system. This will make it much easier for people now driving to work to commute by bicycle. This in turn will help slow the growth in congestion on streets like Monroe and Midvale. The path will ultimately be connected to the Capitol City and Military Ridge Trails providing direct access to an extensive recreational trail network. The path will make it easier for city Parks Department staff to access the area to remove trash and nuisance vegetation. Construction of the path will provide an opportunity to simultaneously correct storm water and drainage problems along the corridor. Access points to the corridor will be made better, and safer.

However, like any project, good planning is needed to ensure that the above mentioned benefits actually accrue to the community. A successful project will minimize and aleviate undesirable impacts, and be an asset to adjacent landowners and neighborhoods. The following statement sums up the committee's basic principles for the path.

Vision Statement:

The trail corridor should safely meet the needs of bicycle commuters, recreational bikers, walkers, in-line skaters, disabled users, and dog owners while being esthetically pleasing to users and neighbors. The corridor should be designed and operated to minimize and alleviate undesirable impacts, preserve compatible uses, and be an asset to adjacent land owners and neighbors.

Goals

The design recommendations that follow shall be consistent with the following specific goals. The goals express overall design attributes and shall be consistent with the Vision Statement.

1. Users of the path include but are not limited to cyclists, pedestrians, pedestrians in wheelchairs, pedestrians with dogs, and in-line skaters. Provide ample space on the path to reduce conflicts between the various users of the path.

2. Provide a safe and direct route for all users to destinations such as work; bus stops; neighbors' homes; area schools including Cherokee School, Edgewood HS and college, and UW campus; businesses including Monroe Street shops and the UW Hospital; and recreation areas including the UW Arboretum and West Mooreland Park.

4. Provide a safe secure environment for those living adjacent to the path and to those using the path.

5. Provide landscaping so as to improve the appearance of the path, improve drainage, enhance neighborsÕ privacy, and provide visual screening as appropriate.

Recomendations

The following are recommendations regarding design and construction of the path. recommendations are subject to physical and engineering constraints.

The path should accommodate only human powered modes of transportation including pedestrians, bicycles, runners/joggers, dog walkers, rollerbladers, wheelchairs, and strollers. Horses and motor vehicles (motorcycles, mopeds, cars, and trucks) should be strictly forbidden. However, motorized devices used to assist the disabled, such as motorized wheelchairs, should not be considered motor vehicles.

While cross country skiers should be allowed to use the transportation corridor containing the path, they should not use the paved path itself and no special accommodations should be made to the corridor by the City of Madison in the winter for skiers. Skiers should have to make their own trail to the side of the paved path.

The transportation corridor containing the path should not be considered an off leash dog park. Dog walkers should be encouraged to walk their leashed dogs to the side of the paved path and should remove dog waste as outlined in City of Madison Ordinances.

Wiring conduits for lighting should be installed along the entire length of the path.

Lighting should be installed south from the Beltline to Lovell Lane as suggested by residents and businesses of the area.

From the Beltline to Breese Terrace lighting should only be installed in areas where most (51% response with 60% of respondents giving approval) of the adjacent homeowneres want it, or where safety requires it. Where lighting is installed, specially selected down-lighting fixtures of comparable expense with shields to prevent light spillage should be installed. A demonstration rig should be used to allow neighbors to view lighting options.

Lighting should be installed from Breese Terrace to Randall Avenue.

Extend funding to allow neighborhood requests for additional lighting for one year following completion of the project.

Lighting installations should allow timed operation subject to neighborhood preferences and safety requirements.

Access points should have low wattage lighting or illuminated signage.

Lighting should be installed south from the Beltline to Lovell Lane as suggested by residents of the area. Lighting should also be installed where traffic safety considerations warrant it including at intersections with streets and public access points on the path.

Where lighting is installed, the light fixtures should minimize light spillage into residential areas. When residential areas abut only one side of the path, lights should be on the residential side of the path and back shields should be used to limit light spillage into the residential areas. This is especially important for lights in the Midvale-to-Beltline segment.

The committee cannot cannot make a recommendation for a specific light fixture at this time. However, a lighting rig should be used to demonstrate different lighting options to neighbors and path users at proposed lighting sites before a fixture is selected.

The committee recommends that the conduit necessary for the lighting electrical cables be installed along the path even where no lighting is to be installed at this time. The operation of the path in the areas without lighting should then be tested for a trial period. Light fixtures can be installed at a later date if the neighbors or path users find that lighting is necessary for safety reasons.
. Wiring conduits for lighting should be installed when the path is built. This installation of lighting on the eastern segment should be the considered at a later time and then initially on an experimental basis where neighbors agree.

The availability of some funds for lighting should be extended beyond the completion date of the path in case additional lighting beyond that described above is found to be necessary.

No fencing or railing should be installed except for the safe operation of the path.

Reflective lane markings should be painted on the path.

Traffic, warning, and street name signs should be installed at appropriate locations.

The police should periodically patrol the path as they do any other road. The patrols would be a great opportunity to take advantage of bicycle police.

The path should be wide enough to accomodate police cars and other emergency vehicles.

The path should be added to the City of Madison's priority bike snow removal route because of the anticpated high level of year round use of the path.

The committee recommends that if deicing compounds are used, they should be environmentally friendly such as calcium magnesium acetate.

The committee strongly encourages the neighborhoods adjacent to the path to form a "Friends of the Southwest Commuter Path" association to mediate concerns and problems with with the corridor, relay concerns to the City, coordinate volunteer efforts to beautify the corridor, and to raise funds to carry out the above activities.

The Hammersley Road overpass of the Beltline Highway should connect to the Odana Golf Course maintenance road via an asphalt/gravel ? path. Alternatively, a crushed limestone path on the railbed should be used if the tracks are removed by the time the overpass is completed.

No improved path should be provided from the maintenance road east to Midvale Boulevard across the City owned greenspace. However, people are free to cut across the greenspace if they choose.

Access to the temporary path from Hammersley Road north of the Beltline via a crushed limestone path should be vigorously pursued.

The paved surface should be ten feet wide except from Commonwealth to Randall Avenue where it should be twelve feet wide.

The paved surface should be asphault or concrete.

The shoulders along the paved surface should be 2-5 feet mowed grass.

Unpaved turnout areas with benches should be provided approximately every one-half mile.

The path should be an asphalt surface a minimum of 10 feet wide to accomodate the many different types of users.
. The path should be a ten to twelve foot paved path with cleared shoulders.

The asphalt surface should be flanked on either side by 2-3 feet of grass to provide a softer surface for walking dogs and runners/joggers. Cross country skiers could use this area in the winter.
. The path should be a minimum width of ten feet when physical constraints allow. It should be twelve feet where possible without excessive engineering. lowering the grade more than one foot.

Drainage areas should be constructed in areas where water is likely to collect and stand. These drainage areas should stand off from the path far enough so that they do not pose any hazard to users of the path. Drainage and landscaping should be managed to minimize mosquito breeding areas. Drainage areas should be placed on the side away from residents in those areas where vacant public land occupies one side of the corridor.

Pedestrian crossing islands should be installed in Midvale and Odana roads.

Traffic lights that stop traffic on Midvale should be installed at the path crossing.

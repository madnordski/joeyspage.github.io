Committee for the Design of the Southwest Commuter Bike and Pedestrian Path, Notes, 11 August 1998

by Joseph King

Now we are one. From the first meeting in February until June, the Committee for the Design of the Southwest Bicycle and Pedestrian Commuter Path was divided into two groups. One group looked at the segment of the proposed rail corridor to path conversion west from Midvale Avenue to Madison's border with Fitchburg. The other group looked at the corridor east of Midvale to where it arrives near the UW campus on Randall Street. During a meeting in June it was decided we would produce a single report. Named to the document committee were the authors of each group's first drafts, Mark Shahan and myself. In addition, Tom Branson, Paul Beckett, and Chris Hagman (east group co-chair) were also named. The document committee made progress on the introduction and goals. They had ready a list of questions to be put before the full committee but the July meeting of the whole was postponed for lack of quorum.

The document committee met again on August 4th to take up the drafting of recommendations on path width, surface, lighting, and other issues. Comments regarding this draft can be sent directly to the document committee at swp-doc@gcg.com or to the entire committee plus other interested citizens at swp@gcg.com. Please keep in mind that these documents are rough drafts and are not, as of yet, the end product of the committee's work. The full committee has approved almost nothing in these documents. Comments relating to these documents will be gratefully received especially if they are worded as kind suggestions.

At the August 11th meeting of the committee of the whole the document committee presented a list of decisions to be taken. The committee formally endorsed the following list of decisions. These decisions will be drafted into specific recommendations. The final recommendations will then be voted on by the committee as part of the final report. So what follows is not to be taken as the final wording of any recommendation. If your browser supports it, the decisions will be colored in blue while my comments are in italics.

  • Opening Statement: The following recommendations concerning the path design and construction are subject to physical and engineering constraints.

    The above statement was amended but I failed to record the final version. The intent of the amendment was to include adjacent landowner improvements to the corridor as a constraint provided safety is not compromised. I could not support this for four reasons. The first was procedural. We have much to do in this meeting and each time we ignore proper meeting procedure will limit our effectiveness as a committee. The second was, the desired goal would be best handled as a recommendation that states respect for existing improvements. The third was, the final wording did not made it clear that we are talking about improvements to the existing right of way and that safety must not be compromised in efforts to preserve these improvements. The fourth was, before we made any such statements I would like to be sure that we are in no way compromising the legal position of the city with regard to the corridor. I would suggest that if we do acknowledge improvements to the corridor that we should explicitly state this intent (e.g. this acknowledgment shall not form the basis for an transfer of property to adjacent property owners, implied or otherwise). I think the drafting committee should come back to the full committee with a recommendation that meets these needs.

  • Path Width: The paved surface should be ten feet wide, except from Commonwealth to Randall Avenue, where it should be twelve feet wide.

    Experience shows that wider paths increase the comfort and safety of pedestrians. Since the section from Commonwealth to campus will likely be the most heavily used, this section should be twelve feet wide.

  • Path Surface: The paved surface of the path should be asphalt or concrete.

    In the past we've had much discussion about path surface. It is clear that only a paved path will be able to serve the ADA and transportation needs of the corridor. In addition, most soft surfaces will still attract many of the same users. Depending on how solid the surface is, it could exclude some cyclists but not all. If the surface is soft enough to exclude most cyclists it would likely be too soft for most, if not all, pedestrians using wheelchairs. In addition, a soft surface will place all users on less secure footing and limit the stopping ability of cyclists. It is my opinion that in addition to reducing the transportation benefit, a soft surface increases the likelihood of accidents.

  • Path Shoulders: The shoulders along the paved surface should be 2-5 feet mowed area where feasible.

    Safety requires a 2 foot shoulder. It is recognized that there are places along the corridor where not even that will be possible and stop gap safety measures will need to be applied (e.g. the installation of railings). The parks department will be cutting the grass and for their purposes five feet is best. While no one has proposed a specific recommendation, it would be appropriate to make it clear that beyond 2 feet additional width must be subject to considerations of safety and existing improvements.

  • Turn Outs: Unpaved turnout areas with benches should be provided approximately every one-half mile.

    I suggested that these turns out be located at the access points when possible. The committee decided to leave the decision as written above. My thinking is that locating the benches near access points has the following benefits. 1. The access points will be the busier portions of the path thus reducing the likelihood of vandalism. 2. By being near the access points they will provide the greatest benefit to the most largest number of users. 3. By locating the turn outs near access points it will avoid confusion and reduce the potential for accidents.

  • Access Point Lighting: Access points should have low wattage lighting or illuminated signage.

  • Funding for Lighting: Funding should be extended to allow neighborhood requests for additional lighting for one year following the completion of the project.

    The document committee has done the most work on ligting. Unfortunately, there was still enough work still to do that we tabled all of the decisions on lighting except for the two that appear above. Our intent is to recommend that lighting be installed in accordance with adjacent land owners wishes provided that minimum safety requirements are met. I have agreed to this in the interest of compromise. For the section of the path from the Beltline to Spooner, this is a very contentious issue. I've made it clear to the committee that lighting the entire corridor would be best for safety on the corridor, safety and security of adjacent homes to the corridor, and for transportation purposes. It is my hope that over time the neighborhoods that do not request lighting initially will realize this and request it later. This would be consistent with past experiences, however, usually there are no funds to meet these requests after construction. City staff indicated at this meeting that despite our recommendation to keep funds for lighting, it is unlikely that there will be funds available for the later installation of lights. This is because the funding agencies generally don't allow the city to hold back funds for use at a later time.

About half of the committee members are adjacent landowners to the corridor. We have also heard from many others that live adjacent to the proposed path. Surprisingly, many of the later group oppose the construction of the path. Objections have be raised to every aspect of path design including a paved surface, the need to clear snow, ADA compliant access points, lighting, a minimum ten foot width, and cleared shoulders. In addition, some have insisted that the project include funds to purchase visual screening for their property. Based on past experiences, however, others fully expect that this path will be an asset to their neighborhood that will enhance their living environment and the value of their homes.

Studies have been presented to the committee that indicate projects such as these either don't change or improve the security of the area. The privacy of homes along the proposed path is already compromised by heavy usage of the existing rail line by able-bodied pedestrians. It has been pointed out that concerns about multiple use are best addressed by increasing the width of the path. In addition, safety standards and the need for alternative transportation are also important. Construction of the path will allow access by community members using wheelchairs and walking aids permitting them to enjoy the natural areas in and along the corridor. The completed project will be an asset to every community it touches by providing a pleasant link within and between some of Madison's most vibrant neighborhoods.

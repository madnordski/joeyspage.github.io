East Subcommittee to the Committee for the Design of the Southwest Bicycle and Pedestrian Commuter Path (SWP)

Proposed Design Recommendations, 30 June 1998

  1. Introduction

    2. This document represents the work product of the East Subcommittee to the Committee for the Design of the Southwest Bicycle and Pedestrian Commuter Path, a formally constituted committee of the City of Madison. This document is divided into two parts. The first part is a normative section that represents the committees design goals and recommendations. The second part is a rationale section that documents concerns raised during the process and how they are addressed.

    1.1 Changes to this Document

    An editor shall be appointed by the committee to oversee changes to this document. The editor will have a free license to make grammar and spelling corrections to the document. All other changes shall be approved by a majority of committee members in attendance at official committee meetings.

    1.2 Vision Statement

    The trail and corridor should safely meet the needs of bicycle commuters, recreational bikers, walkers, skiers, persons with disabilities and dog owners and should be aesthetically pleasing to users and neighbors. The corridor should be designed and operated to minimize and alleviate undesirable impacts, preserve compatible uses and be an asset to adjacent land owners and neighbors.

    1.3 Terminology

    This document refers to the proposed Southwest Bicycle and Pedestrian Commuter Path as the path. The Committee for the Design of the Southwest Bicycle Pedestrian Commuter Path is referred to as the SWP Committee. The term wheeled users is used to describe users of bikes, rollerblades, roller skates, wheelchairs, and other wheeled devices that can be legally used on Madison bike paths.

     

     

  2. Goals

The design recommendations that follow shall be consistent with the following specific goals. The goals express overall design attributes and shall be consistent with the Vision Statement.

  1. of the path including but are not limited to cyclists, pedestrians, pedestrians in wheelchairs, pedestrians with dogs, and roller-bladers. Provide amble space on the path to reduce conflicts between the various users of the path.
  2. Provide a safe and direct route for all users of the path to and from destinations.
  3. Provide access to the path such that destinations would reasonably include but not be limited to work, area schools, commercial businesses, school, the store, bus stops, the UW Arboretum, West Mooreland Park, UW Hospital, UW campus, Monroe Street shops, Cherokee School, Edgewood HS and college and the neighbors house.
  4. Provide a safe secure environment for those living adjacent to the path and to those using the path.
  5. Provide landscaping so as to improve the appearance of the path and reduce mosquito breeding areas.
  6. Enhance neighbors’ privacy.
  7. Provide appropriate visual screening to adjacent lots.

3.0 Recommendations

Design recommendations of the committee are intended to provide guidance to city engineers in the development of a design for the construction of the path. Aspects of design that are not specified should be determined by city engineers in a manner that is consistent with the Goals specified in this document.

  1. The path should be a ten to twelve foot paved path with cleared shoulders.
  2. The path should be a minimum width of ten feet when physical constraints allow. It should be twelve feet where possible without excessive engineering. lowering the grade more than one foot.
  3. Drainage areas should be constructed in areas where water is likely to collect and stand. These drainage areas should stand off from the path far enough so that they do not pose any hazard to users of the path. Drainage and landscaping should be managed to minimize mosquito breeding areas. Drainage areas should be placed on the side away from residents in those areas where vacant public land occupies one side of the corridor.
  4. Pedestrian crossing islands should be installed in Midvale and Odana roads.
  5. Traffic lights that stop traffic on Midvale should be installed at the path crossing.
  6. Wiring conduits for lighting should be installed when the path is built. This installation of lighting on the eastern segment should be the considered at a later time and then initially on an experimental basis where neighbors agree.
  7. Access should be provide at the following points but not limited to these locations:
    • Chippewa Drive
    • Anthony Lane
    • Gregory St. (near Glenwood)
    • Virginia Terrace
    • Sheldon St.
    • Prospect St. and/or Harrison St.
    • Oakland Ave. or Breeze Terrace

 

R.1.0 Rationale

The first section represents the committee’s vision, goals, and recommendations to the City of Madison. This section, the rationale, documents the committee discussions. As a matter of operating procedure we have tried to proceed by consensus, however, even when a consensus is reached there exist differing views and conflicting input from residents. It is the goal of the rationale to summarize these views in a way that illuminates the choices described above. The numbering in this section shall correspond to the numbering of the section to which it refers.

R.2.1 Goals

Although a complete list of specific businesses and schools that would be served by this path would be useful in determining priority access points, we did not attempt to construct such a list. It is noted, however, that certain work places and schools attract high rates of bicycle commuters. Grade schools and the UW campus, for example. In addition, recreational destinations are also noted as important destinations to the users of this path.

It was suggested that a goal of the path should be to enhance neighbors’ privacy and that a goal should be to provide visual screening. To some on the committee these recommendations seem inappropriate. The visual effect for the length of the corridor should be an improvement over existing conditions. Based on similar experiences, the residents along the path are likely to enjoy enhanced property values as a result of the paths’ construction. Visual screening, applied throughout the corridor, would direct public funds toward enhancing those property values further without public benefit. Based on similar experiences, adjacent landowners will be among the primary users of the path and enjoy further benefit with regard to ped/bike access to neighborhood destinations. In addition, the corridor is already heavily used by the public thus path construction will have little impact on existing conditions with regard to privacy. Furthermore, for much of the path there already exists a natural screen that is well beyond that enjoyed by most Madison homeowners not to mention those with corner lots. This is not say that some screening might be appropriate in some instances. Bike lights at night, for example, might be more intrusive than existing conditions.

R.3.0 Recommendations

Path Surface and Width: There was a tremendous amount of discussion about the choice of path surface and width. Several members and interested parties spoke to the need for a gravel path instead of a paved path, presumably in order to create a predominately pedestrian path. A gravel path would likely inhibit bicycle use and eliminate rollerblader use as well as use by people in wheelchairs. It was noted by city staff that gravel paths are more difficult to maintain. It was also noted that it would be difficult to find funding to build a gravel path in this location. A gravel path would be inconsistent with the vision and goals set forth above.

The width of the path may be constrained by the physical attributes of the corridor. Beyond that, some members and residents argued for a narrower path. Presumably, again, to impede use by wheeled users. Observation of other existing paths illustrates the flaw in this thinking. A paved and even a gravel path will still attract wheeled users. If the path is too narrow it will result in an increase in collisions on the path. Current safety standards call for a ten foot path or wider when the path is expected to have the levels of mixed usage expected on this path. The desire to inhibit usage by bicycle commuters and other wheeled users is inconsistent with the vision and goals set forth herein.

Lighting: There was much discussion on lighting. Here too it is difficult to even begin to summarize all of the points of view on lighting. Based on past experience, however, the design should anticipate the need for lighting the entire length of the path. No lighting should be installed without an additional approval process that involves the adjacent landowners directly. Although in most instances lighting will enhance safety for the user of the path and the adjacent landowner a like, we found significant opposition to its installation in certain segments. It is noted that the eastern end of the segment near Breeze Terrace may wish to request lighting as part of the path construction project.

Access Points: Some access points have greater transportation significance than others. It was noted that the Virginia Terrace access point will give direct access to West High School and the UW Hospital. Other parts of the UW campus, as well as businesses on Old University Ave would be served by the access point. It was also noted that this access point might provide some engineering challenge but because of the importance, every effort should be made. Replacing this access point with the crossing of Commonwealth Ave. is clearly unacceptable.

There was some confusion with the listing of Gregory Street as an access point. This is intended to be the section of Gregory that is west of Glenway. This access point provides access from an otherwise isolated neighborhood.

The Prospect Street access point already has access for non-wheeled pedestrians. It is listed here so that it is modified as required for use by wheeled users. As an alternative Harrison Street was suggested. We did not agree which point provided the most significant challenges and so this is left to city engineering to decide. It is noted that Prospect might require more engineering while Harrison is in closer proximity to homes. It is clear that if Monroe Street businesses are to be served by this path, that at least one of these access points are required.

The Oakland Ave. access point might be better designated as Breeze Terrace.

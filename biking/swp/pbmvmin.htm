7. Traffic Circle - New Washburn Way

Persons appearing:

Linda Bellman, Ald. First District, representing the High Point Estate neighborhood area: Since becoming a Council member, she had been plagued with concerns about traffic volume/speed problems on New Washburn Way. Besides neighborhood collector traffic, New Washburn Way was being used as a cut-through to avoid the Watts Road and Mineral Point Road intersection by Woodman's. When Dave Simon came to the City with a development project south of the area, Simon agreed to finance a traffic circle on New Washburn Way to slow and possibly deter traffic from using New Washburn Way. An initial survey was conducted of the immediate area to be impacted with a traffic circle after installation of trial barrels to test the feasibility of a traffic circle at New Washburn Way, Dandaneau and Chautauqua. Because of the conflict which resulted, the issue was put on hold so that it could be relooked at and a neighborhood committee was formed. A survey distributed by the neighborhood association of the entire neighborhood was conducted. The results of the survey indicated a similar percentage of supportC61.5% for the survey of the immediate area and 61.2% for the larger group. She supported moving forward with constructing the traffic circle although she acknowledged it would not address all of the traffic concerns. She thanked Bill Scheuerell for his efforts in distributing and processing the survey results.

William Scheuerell (7334 New Washburn Way), appeared in support echoing comments made by Ald. Bellman.

Kathy Andersen (7502 New Washburn Way), appeared in support, pointing out that current conditions supported the notion that the street was being used as a cut through to avoid the Watts Road area. She felt the Simon development would bring more traffic so anything that could be done to minimize traffic impacts would be appreciated.

Shahan asked Andersen if she saw the school bus stop of concern; she responded that she would not think it would be an issue if the stop was located such that it was at the approach to the circle.

William McAweeney (6 Chautauqua Trail) opposed a traffic circle. He referred to the stop sign already located on New Washburn some 400 yards away and suggested if speeding were the problem and if this stop sign hadn't made a difference, why would a traffic circle. He didn't feel speeding was an issue although he acknowledged that volumes had increased and would likely increase with the Simon development.

Dryer stated at one of the first meetings, the T-intersection with the stop signs was a part of the discussion. Staff had indicated that the stop signs would probably not have much influence on vehicle speeds, and one could expect motorists running stop signs in a situation such as this. This was borne out with experience reported by residents on New Washburn Way. Stop signs were not seen as a sustainable traffic control measure for vehicle speeds. The idea of the traffic circle is that as an active traffic control device it required motorists to slow down to move around the circleCbasically a self-enforcing device. It also allows for improved landscaping in the neighborhood and traffic circles have been fairly well accepted throughout the country. Traffic circles were seen as a sustainable form of traffic control.

Kaysen agreed that a reduction of 5 mph in a residential area is significant, particularly if one considers the impact of a vehicle hitting a person at the lower speed and she thought it would be a good location for a traffic circle.

Shahan said the survey results showed a 2.5-3 mph speed reduction and pointed out with the pedestrian crossings at the intersection, any speed reduction was significant.

Motion by Holtzman/Palmer to approve the report and its recommendations carried unanimously.

9. ID 25494, Resolution accepting the Report of the Committee for the Design of the Southwest Commuter Bike and Pedestrian Path and directing various City Agencies to prepare for the design and operation of this transportation corridor

Persons registering and/or appearing in support were:

 

Ald. Linda Bellman, representing District 1, registered in support.

 

Laura Brown (607 Piper Drive) referred to her e-mail and cited her excitement about the proposed path. She saw it as a tremendous opportunity for all people to get out and bicycle either recreationally or for other purposes. She requested that the path be paved and plowed; otherwise it would be unusable during certain times of the year. Access points for the path needed to be adequate and not out of the way. For herself, the most convenient access point would be the parking lot at the Odana golf course and she didn't understand why there would be any objection to such access. She repeated an offer to assist in placing native plants/plantings along the path.

 

Paul Beckett (2533 Gregory Street), representing the Dudgeon Monroe Neighborhood Association (DMNA) indicated he was a member of the citizens committee developing the report and urged its passage. DMNA has as much property along the tracks as any neighborhood association and they had formed a committee, which had been actively meeting with neighbors. There were four issues he wanted to address. First, on page 5 where the width of the path was discussed, he emphasized the need to allow for flexibility due to physical constraints in some locations. He urged support of this committee recommendation for flexibility. Engineers would generally speak to a consistent and uniform width, and would argue for the safety considerations to maintain such uniformity, but he felt the PBMVC could consider and support the recommendation relative to flexibility. For example, in DMNA neighborhood there were some houses that were just a few feet from the edge of the rail right-of-way and whose yards would be severely impacted by the grading necessary if the 10 ft. width had to be maintained for the entire path with 5 ft. shoulders on each side. Next, he referred to amenities and urged the Commission to emphasize what is called for in the report. The third point dealt with access points and he urged they look at everything including Zook park. Finally, he expressed strong sentiment from the neighborhood against winter plowing.

 

Bowser asked for clarification about the width concerns; was the concern not necessarily with the width of the path, but rather the width of the shoulders? Beckett responded that was true. If they insisted on 10 ft. wide paths with 5 ft. shoulders, it would represent a 20 ft. wide corridor. But, when one stabilized grades, it could mean that as much as 24-25 ft. would be needed.

 

Bowser asked why the position on snow plowing knowing that many people liked to bike during the winter. Beckett responded that a lot of neighborhood people felt that it should be available for skiing. They also opposed using salt which would be expected to run off into Lake Wingra.

 

Holtzman referred to item 6 in the resolution which identified shoulders as being 2-5 ft. wide and he asked if this addressed the concern about flexibility; Beckett responded it would.

 

Shahan said one thought relative to plowing was to try to accommodate both skiers and bicyclists by plowing a narrower path.

 

Robbie Webber (2613 Stevens Street) saw the path as a very important transportation corridor and alternative to use of streets such as Monroe and Regent Streets. As the Bike-to-Work-Week Coordinator, she said safe routes were listed as one of the most important things by persons and is stated as a limiting factor for not riding more. Their web page had a route planning request and today she had received a request about using the track area between Odana and Johnson. She emphasized it was a transportation corridor and not just a recreational path; and for that reason, she said it needed to be paved and plowed. She said it should be to AASHTO standards according to width and other features. Although a cross country skier herself, she saw a usable bike path a greater need since Madison didn't receive enough snow to allow for regular skiing. She encouraged frequent access points. She was impressed with the committee's work.

 

Darin Burleigh (5825 Raymond Road #4) similarly saw the corridor as an effective transportation corridor and he referred to some of the points already raised. As a bike commuter, he saw it as a wonderful opportunity for him to reach points downtown. He appreciated the access/connection points. He hoped to be a winter commuter and because of that would like to see the path plowed and possibly lightly sanded when necessary. He thought the idea of providing native plantings was great. A safe, effective path to get people from place to place was desired.

 

Shahan asked if Burleigh saw the path being used by pedestrians along with bicyclists; Burleigh responded that it could be, that multi-purpose was good, but then it would have to be wide enough to accommodate all uses.

 

Michael Barrett (2137 Sommers), representing BTA, pointed out multi-use paths have a pretty poor safety record, due to a number of factorsCnumber of beginners, variety of speeds, variety of users, path design, etc. He was not pointing this out as a negative to bike paths but rather as an argument to engineer the path to accommodate the variety of users; including, adequate width for roller bladers, wheel chair users, strollers and bike trailers, and the volume of users. Formal access points are important for safety. Clear sight lines at clearly designated access points were essential. The report contained recommendations relative to these issues and he thanked the advisory committee and staff for their good, thoughtful work.

 

Pamela Barrett (2137 Sommers Avenue) cited her enthusiasm for the path. She saw it as a great asset for the neighborhood, too; a place to get out and interact with other neighbors. She felt the width standards needed to be adhered to because of the multi users. Access points, were a major issue and they should be dealt with as the path was built rather than later. It was important for the path to be accessible throughout the seasons and therefore should be plowed. She did not believe salt had to be used, rather a sand mixture.

 

Daryl Sherman (3106 Gregory) supported the path with reservations. He said he was a three-season bike commuter and his home backed-up on the path's right-of-way. He had attended many meetings about the path and use of this corridor. His reservation dealt with the width of path. In the area of Glenway and Commonwealth, one will find that the surface now is only 10 ft. wide. According to Tony Fernandez, design engineer, to provide for a path at the width called for in the report would require bulldozing an area 27 ft. wide in certain places. He thought it was excessive and urged that any bulldozing be minimized. He suggested instead they use the existing road bed and just provide for a narrower path in these areas. He noted his experience using the 8 ft. wide Brittingham path and he felt it was adequate. He referred also to the Wingra path as another example of a narrower path. He suggested in the narrower sections, users would just need to use more caution as they moved through the area. He asked that they minimize the impact or destruction on mature trees and vegetation in the area of the path. In summary, he proposed an 8 ft. wide permeable paved surface with no more than 30" shoulders (preferred 2 ft.). He did not support initially plowing the path, and certainly not salting it in the winter.

 

Holtzman asked if Sherman saw the report as a compromise to the many views, including those he was expressing. Sherman responded that the neighborhood association had appointed three people to attend and he was not one of them. Accordingly, he had not attended those meetings, rather attended the public meetings scheduled by Golden and the neighborhood association. He realized there were compromises in the report, but he did not feel he had had a chance to lay out in detail his reasons as he had just done. However, Shahan reported those comments were heard by the committee. Shahan asked if Sherman had data to support an 8 ft. path vs. a 10 ft. wide path. The data he had seen indicated 8 ft. is not as safe; and in fact, Portland, where they have heavily used paths, they were moving to 12 ft. paths. Sherman responded his comments were based on his experience.

 

Kaysen asked about Sherman's comments about the impact of noise from plowing and asked how the noise of trains had impacted householders. Sherman responded that trains went by just once a week on Wednesday nights and they had learned to live with it so it wasn't a critical problem.

 

Vedder asked about his comments on permeable surface. Sherman said he did not have data but in their neighborhood council they had a person who is a director for the Arboretum. The Arboretum is presently looking into putting down permeable paths (hard surface paths). More details about the process were to be presented at an upcoming meeting. He thought up to 48% of their neighborhood is now comprised of impermeable surfaces and saw no need to add to this if there was an alternative; and therefore, he was asking that it be looked at. Shahan asked that he share any information he received on permeable surfaces.

 

Alfred Matano (718 Eugenia Avenue), representing the Sierra Club, thanked those who participated in putting together the report. It was seen as a positive transportation alternative. Besides it providing an in-town route, it would provide a safe and enjoyable route for persons out of the City and eventual plans were for it to connect to a trail that would go to Freeport, Illinois. He saw paving and plowing important since it would be a transportation corridor. Since the path would be built on a rail corridor, if the need arose, it could be converted back to provide for commuter rail.

 

As an aside, Matano said he had intended to be present at the start of the meeting so that he could mention another concern; and he provided a copy of a letter he had written and responses received relative to the closing of the bike lane on University Avenue for a construction project. Due to the UW's project, the bike lane was being shutdown in the latter part of the construction project. The closing of the bike lane had been approved by staff, but there had been no public input. He asked that the Commission consider it and share some of their concerns with the UW. Bike corridors needed to be taken as seriously as automobile corridors.

 

Kathryn Lederhause (3106 Gregory Street) indicated she was a property owner backing up on the rail track corridor. She echoed comments already expressed, especially those of Mr. Sherman. She wanted to see some modifications in the Glenway and Commonwealth area. It is the area where the right-of-way is elevated, and the top surface is narrower. She understood it would require up to 27 ft. of bulldozing. There is a bridge in the section which was to be kept so already the path would be narrowed to 8 ft. at that point with 2 ft. shoulders. She requested they provide an 8 ft. section between Glenway-Commonwealth. Referring to the comments about the report referencing flexibility, she stated that in meetings with Tony Fernandez and Christy Bachman, they felt their requests were falling on deaf ears. She objected to plowing, not only because it would be in her backyard, but also because of the increased noise and air pollution. She saw no problem with bicyclists not using the path during inclement periods. She referred to the excellent bus system which could serve as a transportation mode alternative. She did not favor lighting in that section of the path.

 

Shahan referred to Lederhause's comments about plowing to accommodate bicyclists and asked what about accommodating pedestrian use; wouldn't it be the same as sidewalks.

 

Kaysen referred to no. 8 on the resolution which indicated there shouldn't be lighting unless most of the adjacent land owners requested, and Kaysen asked if this wasn't a sufficient safeguard. Lederhause said if it were followed, it would be sufficient.

 

Persons registering in support and not speaking: Charles W. Strawser (1142 Jenifer); Tom Branson (10 Nokomis Court); Scott Sauer (4302) Clover Court, representing self and a member of the citizens committee; David Drummon (208 Lathrop), representing the SW Path Committee;

 

Kennedy explained that the action to be taken would not pass on the design of the facility; rather it would accept the report of the committee and direct City agencies to prepare for the design and operation. The design and operation would be brought back at a later time when more specifics were known.

 

Kaysen/Holtzman moved adoption of the report and resolution.

Kaysen highlighted the issues raised about access points and knew that people would make their own access points if adequate ones were not provided. The issue of access needed to be critically considered in the design phase. The report from the Parks Division about not using the parking lot from the Odana Road golf course confused her. It was as if Parks was a separate resource and not a part of the City of Madison and community being served. This access was being used now and would continue to be whether or not it were made an official access point. In some of the steeper places, it would be critical to plan in the access points now.

 

Bowser asked if the plans and specifications would be approved by the Board of Public Works; Nelson responded yes. Would people be notified of the hearings? Nelson explained the City had retained Earth Tech to do the design and they had completed the field surveys for the path and begun the design. They met that day for the first design meeting. Because of the public participation in the process, they would schedule a regular monthly design meeting. The City's goal was to have the plans, specifications and estimated costs completed by February 1, 2000, with bidding to occur in May so construction could commence. For that reason, there wasn't a lot of time to take on controversial access points. He had urged the Ald. and Mr. Fernandez not to become embroiled in it. Based on other experience, they felt the neighborhood would be very concerned about certain accesses and later if they were not provided staff would be criticized for not putting them. The path did not represent particularly expensive construction or a complicated process. The width for the path was 10 ft. with 5 ft. shoulders provided although this could go down to 2 ft. shoulders.

 

Bowser referred to the areas in which people had expressed concern about the width, trees and shoulder. It sounded as if they would be able to work around most areas of concern.

 

Shahan said the most problematic area was near Commonwealth and a few hundred yards southwest of there. He understood they could get a 10 ft. paved path over the bridge or would the path over the bridge be 8 ft.? Nelson said he wasn't sure about the width, but he did not think it would be a problem. Shahan understood Fernandez to say that a 10 ft. path could be provided with a 2 ft. shoulder yet a speaker was indicating 8 ft. Nelson said the bridge had caused a lot of discussion and it would need to be sorted out. There were actually two bridges built there, and they would need to address the issue.

 

Palmer asked if there were any examples of permeable surfaces. Nelson said they haven't used any in the City. Permeable pavement has not been successful in northern climates.

However, here was some discussion about using the trackage between Regent and Randall for train storage.

Nelson responded that the snow plow operations were done by the Streets Division for the most part but a number of agencies have specific assignments for plowing some areas. The Engineering Division has the Starkweather Creek and MATC bike paths and the Parks Division does Wingra bike path. On the basis of assignments, the Engineering Division would clear this path and they were looking at different equipment than they now use. They were looking at a smaller, enclosed vehicle which is quite narrow which has a blade and broom. Most cleaning efforts can be done with a broom. Abrasives were used on the paths; it consists of sand and does include a percentage of salt to keep the sand from chunking. He saw these issues not design features, but operation and something they would have to see how it works out. They would not want the neighbors to think that there would be demands for a higher levels of service, which had occurred on the east corridor bike path and which had resulted in more salt use because of primarily the pedestrian use of the path.

Dryer reported that Farley Avenue was a project in which staff had been working with the Regent Neighborhood.
